## 1. Executive Summary

This report details the development of a Bionic Shrimp-Shell Joint. Inspired by the abdominal pleon of crustaceans, this joint utilizes a rigid-soft coupled architecture to achieve high flexibility and impact resistance. The design addresses the cost barriers of traditional humanoid robotics by targeting a production cost of under $800 USD, leveraging underactuated mechanics and composite materials.

## 2. Design Philosophy & Biomimicry

Traditional robotics often rely on "hard" joints (servos and harmonic drives) which are susceptible to impact damage and high energy consumption. The Shrimp-Shell design mimics the arthrodial membrane and overlapping chitinous plates found in shrimp. This allows for a distributed stress model where the mechanical load is shared across multiple segments rather than a single point of failure.

## 3. Theoretical Framework

### 3.1 Multi-Body Kinematics

The joint is modeled as 3-5 segments. Unlike a single-axis hinge, the total angular displacement $\Theta$ is distributed across $n$ segments. Each segment contributes a local rotation $\theta_i$:

$$\Theta = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \theta_i$$

This mathematical distribution reduces the required torque per segment and enables hyper-flexible range of motion (RoM), facilitating postures such as a 90°+ backbend and 180°+ splits.

### 3.2 Reverse Conjugate Surface (RCS) Geometry

To maintain structural stability during rotation, the overlapping surfaces utilize Reverse Conjugate Surface (RCS) design. If $S_1$ and $S_2$ represent the manifold surfaces of adjacent segments $i$ and $i+1$, the contact interface is defined by the transformation:

$$\mathbf{r}_1(u, v) = \mathbf{M} \mathbf{r}_2(s, t)$$

Where $\mathbf{M}$ is a time-varying transformation matrix. This ensures the rolling contact remains continuous, maximizing transmission efficiency and preventing "catching" between shells during high-speed motion.

### 3.3 Tendon Dynamics & Underactuation

The system is driven by Dyneema cables (high-modulus polyethylene) integrated with a spring-return mechanism. The tension $T$ required for actuation is calculated as:

$$T = k \Delta x + f_{friction}(\mu, N)$$

Where $k$ is the spring constant and $f_{friction}$ is a function of the coefficient of friction $\mu$ and normal force $N$ at the segment interfaces. To handle the complex, non-linear dynamics of this multi-segment system, a Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) reinforcement learning model is used for motion control.

## 5. Implementation & Simulation

### 5.1 Mechanical Assembly

The V6 iteration consists of 4–5 rigid composite shell segments. These segments are routed with Dyneema tendons through internal precision pulleys, ensuring a clean exterior profile while protecting the "neural" cabling of the joint.

### 5.2 Physics Validation

The design underwent rigorous testing in the MuJoCo physics engine. Key validation areas included:

* Zero-Shot Stability: Maintaining balance during unexpected torque variations.
* Collision Dynamics: Assessing shell-on-shell impact during rapid contraction.
* Wear Modeling: Simulating tendon fatigue and implementing self-adaptive tension compensation.

## 6. Risk Management

* Tendon Fatigue: Mitigation via low-friction Dyneema and real-time tension monitoring algorithms.
* Manufacturing Precision: Use of 3D-printed composite structures to maintain low costs while ensuring RCS surface tolerances.
* Adoption Barriers: Providing open-source MuJoCo models to the research community to prove reliability.

## 7. Conclusion & Roadmap

The Bionic Shrimp-Shell Joint represents a departure from traditional robotic design toward a more organic, resilient, and cost-effective paradigm. By 2026, the focus will shift from the V6 prototype to full-scale integration in humanoid platforms designed for rescue, education, and healthcare.
