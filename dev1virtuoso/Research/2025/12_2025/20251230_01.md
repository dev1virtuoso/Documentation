## Executive Summary

This report presents three conceptual designs for innovative integrated motor solutions, targeting a cost range of 30-50 yuan (approximately $4-7 USD) per unit in DIY or small-scale production. These designs focus on compact, efficient motors suitable for robotics, automation, and wearable devices. All specifications, performance metrics, and costs provided are estimates based on conceptual engineering analysis, material availability, and prototyping assumptions. No physical prototypes have been built or tested; data are derived from simulations, material properties, and comparative benchmarks from existing technologies.

The designs include:
1. 3D-Printed Flexible Harmonic Reduction BLDC Motor
2. Spring Energy Recovery Axial Flux Motor
3. Low-Voltage Ferroelectric Electrohydraulic Artificial Muscle Motor

Estimated benefits include reduced manufacturing costs through additive manufacturing and recycled materials, improved energy efficiency, and enhanced compliance for soft robotics. Potential challenges involve material durability and scaling for mass production.

## Introduction

### Background
Traditional motors for robotics and automation often exceed budget constraints in low-volume applications, with costs driven by precision machining and specialized components. This report explores conceptual innovations leveraging 3D printing, recycled materials, and novel actuation mechanisms to achieve high performance at low cost (target: 30-50 yuan).

### Objectives
- Design motors with compact size (Ø25-35mm x 35-50mm), weight (100-150g), and torque (1.2-1.5 N·m).
- Ensure compatibility with common controllers (e.g., Arduino, Raspberry Pi).
- Emphasize innovations in cost reduction and energy efficiency.
- All data are estimates; real-world validation would require prototyping.

### Scope and Assumptions
- Focus on conceptual designs; no empirical testing.
- Costs assume DIY access to 3D printers and basic electronics.
- Performance estimates based on finite element analysis (FEA) simulations and literature (e.g., BLDC efficiency ~75-85%).
- Currency: Costs in yuan (CNY), based on 2025 market estimates for materials in China.

## Design Concepts

### 1. 3D-Printed Flexible Harmonic Reduction BLDC Motor

#### Structure Overview
This design integrates a frameless BLDC stator with a 3D-printed flexible harmonic reducer. The stator uses recycled copper wire windings on a 3D-printed PLA core. The harmonic reducer features a TPU-printed flexible cup (flexspline) meshing with a PETG-printed rigid spline and an ABS-printed wave generator. An integrated flexible PCB encoder uses a recycled optical sensor for position feedback. The assembly is housed in a ventilated PLA shell, with a carbon-fiber-reinforced nylon output shaft.

- **Key Components**:
- BLDC Stator: Ø25mm x 20mm, 12 slots, 50 turns per slot.
- Harmonic Reducer: Reduction ratio 50:1, flexspline tooth count 100.
- Encoder: 1024 pulses/rev, optical type.
- Output: Ø6mm shaft with keyway.

#### Estimated Specifications
- Volume: Ø30mm x 40mm (estimated based on CAD modeling).
- Weight: 150g (80g printed parts, 70g windings/magnets).
- Torque: Continuous 1.5 N·m, peak 2.5 N·m (FEA-estimated from magnetic flux density).
- Speed: 0-200 RPM post-reduction.
- Efficiency: ~75% (BLDC 85%, reducer 65%).
- Response Time: <0.1s (from simulation of PWM ramp-up).
- Power: 12V DC, peak 2A.
- Control: PWM-compatible with Raspberry Pi/Arduino.

#### Innovations
- Use of TPU for the flexspline reduces material costs by 70% compared to metal equivalents and provides inherent vibration damping (modulus ~20MPa).
- Integrated encoder eliminates external sensors, simplifying assembly.
- DIY-friendly: Open-source 3D models and recycled copper lower barriers to entry.

#### Estimated Costs
- 3D Printing Materials (TPU/PLA/ABS): 15 yuan.
- Recycled Copper Wire and Magnets: 10 yuan.
- Flexible PCB and Sensor: 10 yuan.
- Miscellaneous (screws, wires): 5 yuan.
- **Total Estimated Cost**: 40 yuan (DIY scale).

#### Potential Challenges
- Durability: TPU flexspline estimated at 5000 hours; could be enhanced with fiber additives.
- Precision: Print accuracy ±0.1mm may affect gear meshing; requires calibration.

### 2. Spring Energy Recovery Axial Flux Motor

#### Structure Overview
An axial flux configuration with PCB-based stators and a magnet-embedded aluminum rotor. Energy recovery is achieved via a spring array (6 compression springs) coupled to the output shaft through a 3D-printed nylon ratchet mechanism. The PCB stator has multilayer copper traces forming coils, fixed in a cylindrical shell. No external gearbox; springs provide dynamic torque tuning.

- **Key Components**:
- Stator: Ø30mm PCB, 8 poles, 30 turns per pole.
- Rotor: Ø32mm aluminum disk, 4 pole pairs.
- Springs: Ø5mm x 15mm, 10N/mm each.
- Ratchet: One-way mechanism for energy capture/release.

#### Estimated Specifications
- Volume: Ø35mm x 35mm.
- Weight: 120g (40g PCB, 50g springs/magnets, 30g shell).
- Torque: Continuous 1.3 N·m, peak 2.0 N·m (with spring assist).
- Speed: 0-500 RPM.
- Efficiency: Base 80%, with 50% energy recovery in cyclic tasks (simulation-based).
- Response Time: <0.05s (instant spring release).
- Power: 24V DC, peak 1.5A.
- Control: CAN bus, with Hall sensor feedback.

#### Innovations
- Spring array enables kinetic energy storage during deceleration, recycling up to 50% for acceleration—ideal for oscillatory applications.
- Axial flux design maximizes torque density without rare-earth magnets (using N42-grade estimates).
- No bearings: 3D-printed bushings reduce friction and cost.

#### Estimated Costs
- PCB Stator (batch): 15 yuan.
- Springs (hardware store): 5 yuan.
- Magnets and Aluminum: 10 yuan.
- 3D-Printed Parts: 5 yuan.
- **Total Estimated Cost**: 35 yuan.

#### Potential Challenges
- Fatigue: Springs estimated at 10,000 cycles; damping fluid could mitigate oscillations.
- Tuning: Preload adjustments needed for specific loads.

### 3. Low-Voltage Ferroelectric Electrohydraulic Artificial Muscle Motor

#### Structure Overview
A biomimetic actuator using a ferroelectric polymer (PVDF-TrFE) bladder filled with silicone oil, actuated by an integrated piezoelectric micropump. Contraction/expansion simulates muscle movement, converted to rotary torque via a 3D-printed helical screw. The bladder is multilayered for strength, housed in a flexible silicone shell with printed silver electrodes.

- **Key Components**:
- Bladder: 20x40mm x 0.1mm thick polymer film.
- Micropump: Ø10mm piezoelectric, low-viscosity oil medium.
- Conversion: Helical screw for linear-to-rotary output.
- Safety: Pressure relief valve.

#### Estimated Specifications
- Volume: Ø25mm x 50mm.
- Weight: 100g (50g bladder, 30g pump/oil, 20g shell).
- Torque: Continuous 1.2 N·m, peak 1.8 N·m.
- Contraction: 0-30% volume change.
- Efficiency: ~60% (high electrostatic, but hydraulic losses).
- Response Time: <0.2s (pump activation simulation).
- Power: 5V DC (boosted to 4.9kV), <1W.
- Control: Analog I/O with pressure feedback.

#### Innovations
- Low-voltage drive (4.9kV vs. traditional 10kV+) via nanofiller-enhanced polymer, enabling safe, compact operation.
- Electrohydraulic mechanism provides soft compliance for human-interaction robotics.
- Biomimetic form factor allows flexible mounting.

#### Estimated Costs
- Polymer Film: 20 yuan.
- Micropump and Oil: 15 yuan.
- 3D-Printed Shell and Electrodes: 5 yuan.
- Miscellaneous: 5 yuan.
- **Total Estimated Cost**: 45 yuan.

#### Potential Challenges
- Lifespan: Polymer estimated at 2000 hours; coatings could improve moisture resistance.
- Leakage: Hydraulic seals critical; prototyping needed.
